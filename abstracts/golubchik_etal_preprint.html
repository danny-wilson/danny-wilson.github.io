Genomic diversity of Staphylococcus aureus in singly-colonized nasal carriers and the implications for detecting recent transmission

Golubchik, T., Batty, E. M., Young, B. C., Fung, R., Larner-Svennson, H., Votintseva, A., Miller, R. R., Godwin, H., Knox, K., Everitt, R. G., Iqbal, Z., Rimmer, A. J., Cule, M., Ip C. L. C., Didelot, X., Harding, R. M., Donnelly, P. J., Peto, T. E., Wilson, D. J., Crook, D. W. and R. Bowden
In preparation for Genome Research. (pdf)

Genetic variation arising within individual hosts provides the raw material for evolution in obligate parasites such as Staphylococcus aureus, a life-threatening bacterial pathogen of humans associated with hospital acquisition. Novel mutation within hosts may play an important role in the progression of disease, while the accrual of mutations during colonization may facilitate surveillance and reconstruction of transmission within hospitals and the community. Here we used Illumina sequencing to investigate the genetic variation within 131 S. aureus genomes sampled from 13 asymptomatic, singly colonized nasal carriers, including five carriers of methicillin resistant S. aureus (MRSA). We discovered 162 single nucleotide polymorphisms and 22 short insertions/deletions among ten hosts, revealing that microvariation is a common feature of nasal carriage. Structural variation in the S. aureus genome associated with insertion or excision of mobile elements was also apparent. We found reduced diversity associated with MRSA carriage and recent use of antibiotics. The fitness landscape during colonization was dominated by purifying selection. Through an analysis of allele frequencies, we detected fluctuating population dynamics during S. aureus carriage, including evidence for population expansion and crashes. Based on our observations, we developed a simple model for the likelihood of recent transmission given the degree of genomic divergence, inferring that one pair of MRSA carriers in the study may have been related by as few as three transmission events. This work provides a foundation for future genomic studies of local transmission in S. aureus, and for comparisons of within-host evolution during invasive disease.
